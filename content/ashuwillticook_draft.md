# Ashuwillticook Rail Trail — Content Draft

## Trail Metadata
- **Name:** Ashuwillticook Rail Trail
- **Name etymology:** From Mahican language: "at the in-between pleasant river" — the pleasant river between the hills (referring to the Hoosic River running between the Greylock and Hoosac ranges)
- **Region:** Berkshires
- **Route:** Adams → Cheshire → Lanesborough → Pittsfield (Merrill Road)
- **Length:** 14.05 miles (22.61 km) — includes all extensions through 2024
- **Surface:** Paved
- **Railroad origin:** Pittsfield & North Adams Railroad (1845); acquired by Western Railroad during construction; became Boston & Albany's North Adams Branch; New York Central Railroad (1900); Boston & Maine Corporation (1981–1990)
- **Year service ended:** 1990
- **Year opened as trail:** 2001 (first phase), 2004 (second phase), 2017 (1.2-mile northern extension), 2022 (1.5-mile southern extension to Crane Ave, Pittsfield), 2024 (0.5-mile extension to Merrill Road, $2.3 million)

## History Narrative (~350 words)
*[Cite [1] for railroad history and name etymology, [2] for trail opening dates]*

The Hoosac Valley is a long green corridor through the northern Berkshires, held between the shoulders of Mount Greylock to the east and the Hoosac Range to the west. The Hoosic River runs through the valley floor, which is why the Mahican people called this place *ashuwillticook* — the pleasant river between the hills.<sup>[1]</sup> When the Pittsfield & North Adams Railroad began laying track through this valley in 1845, it was entering one of the oldest-settled and most industrialized landscapes in western Massachusetts.

The railroad connected the mill towns of Adams and Cheshire — known for paper mills, marble quarrying, and textile manufacturing — to Pittsfield, the Berkshire County seat and a growing industrial city.<sup>[1]</sup> The line aimed higher, too: the original promoters envisioned extending it north to Rutland, Vermont, threading through the mountains to reach new markets. That ambition was never fully realized. The Western Railroad acquired the line during construction, and it eventually became part of the Boston & Albany's North Adams Branch — later passing to the New York Central in 1900 and finally to the Boston & Maine Corporation in 1981.<sup>[1]</sup>

Through all these corporate changes, the valley's mills churned along. Adams was a major paper-producing town; Cheshire had once supplied the famous "Cheshire Mammoth Cheese" to President Jefferson. The railroad served this industrial fabric for over a century. Boston & Maine operated the line until 1990, when service finally ceased.<sup>[1]</sup>

The trail was built in phases, opening its first segments in 2001 and 2004, with extensions added in 2017, 2022, and most recently 2024.<sup>[2]</sup> It runs along the Hoosic River and the edge of Cheshire Reservoir, past former station sites in Adams and Cheshire, with the flanking mountains always visible on either side. In Pittsfield, a few miles from the southern end of the trail, Herman Melville wrote *Moby-Dick* at his farm Arrowhead between 1850 and 1851. He claimed the silhouette of Greylock, visible from his writing desk, inspired the great white whale.<sup>[4]</sup> William Cullen Bryant was born in Cummington, just over the hills to the east. The Berkshires have always been literary country.

## Historical Sites

### Site 1: Adams Station (Trail Trailhead)
- **Lat/Lng:** 42.6220, -73.1176
- **Description:** The Adams trailhead preserves the former railroad station building, now restored and serving as a trail access point. Beside it stands a historic Hoosac Valley Line passenger railcar — BRMX 1444 — on permanent outdoor display. The Adams station was the industrial heart of the northern Berkshires rail network, serving the paper mills and marble quarries that defined the town's identity through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adams,_Massachusetts
- **Image available:** "Adams station 043.JPG" and "BRMX 1444 at Adams station" (Wikimedia Commons)

### Site 2: Cheshire Reservoir
- **Lat/Lng:** 42.5670, -73.1460
- **Description:** The trail follows the eastern edge of Cheshire Reservoir for a long stretch through Cheshire, with views across open water to the hills beyond. The reservoir was created in the nineteenth century to support the town's industrial water needs. Cheshire was also the home of the famous "Cheshire Mammoth Cheese" — a 1,235-pound wheel of cheese presented to President Jefferson in 1802, a gift from the town's dairy farmers that became a national curiosity.
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheshire_Mammoth_Cheese

### Site 3: View toward Mount Greylock
- **Lat/Lng:** 42.5900, -73.1300
- **Description:** From multiple points along the trail's central section through Cheshire and Lanesborough, Mount Greylock — at 3,491 feet the highest peak in Massachusetts — is visible to the east. Greylock is the subject of Thoreau's "A Walk to Wachusett" and was celebrated by Nathaniel Hawthorne, Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr., and Henry David Thoreau. Melville dedicated *Pierre, or The Ambiguities* to Greylock "in all his benignity." The mountain dominates the visual landscape of this entire trail.
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Greylock

## In-Development Trails

### Ashuwillticook Rail Trail — Continued Southern Extension (Pittsfield)
- **Status:** Active development (most recent extension completed 2024; further extensions planned)
- **Description:** The trail's most recent 2024 extension pushed the southern terminus to Merrill Road in Pittsfield at a cost of $2.3 million. The trail is anticipated to continue southward deeper into Pittsfield, which has been actively investing in downtown trail connections. Future phases would connect the trail to Pittsfield's urban core and potentially link to other Berkshire trail corridors.
- **Link:** https://www.berkshireeagle.com/news/northern_berkshires/rail-trail-extension-pittsfield-adams-merrill-road/article_af3bfc0a-ca1b-11ef-934b-8f073f6d44b8.html

### Berkshire Greenway (Long-term Vision)
- **Status:** Planning/advocacy phase
- **Description:** Various Berkshire County planning and advocacy efforts envision a "Berkshire Greenway" concept — a connected trail network running the length of Berkshire County from the Vermont border south to Connecticut, incorporating the Ashuwillticook and other existing corridors. The Berkshire Regional Planning Commission and local trail advocates have been building toward this vision incrementally. No single funded project spans the full corridor yet.
- **Link:** https://storymaps.arcgis.com/stories/dc69c1d06f2a44c19e7df703294c2349
