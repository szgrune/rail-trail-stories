# Norwottuck Rail Trail — Content Draft

## Trail Metadata
- **Name:** Norwottuck Rail Trail (part of the Mass Central Rail Trail system)
- **Region:** Pioneer Valley
- **Route:** Northampton → Hadley → Amherst → Belchertown
- **Length:** 11 miles (18 km)
- **Surface:** Paved (widened from 8 to 10 feet in 2013–2015 rehabilitation)
- **Railroad origin:** Central Massachusetts Railroad (opened this section 1887); leased to Boston & Maine Railroad as the "Central Massachusetts Branch"
- **Year passenger service ended:** April 23, 1932
- **Year freight service ended:** ~1979 (paper mill closure)
- **State acquired corridor:** 1980
- **Year opened as trail:** July 29, 1993

⚠️ **PRD Correction:** The PRD listed "New Haven & Northampton Railroad (1853)" as the origin railroad. After research, the trail actually follows the Central Massachusetts Railroad's Norwottuck branch, which opened in 1887. The New Haven & Northampton Railroad ran a different corridor. History below reflects the corrected attribution.

## History Narrative (~350 words)
*[Cite [1] for railroad history, [2] for trail opening, [3] for name etymology]*

The Connecticut River Valley has always been the spine of western Massachusetts — its farms, its colleges, its small cities arranged along the floodplain like beads on a string. When the Central Massachusetts Railroad extended its tracks through this corridor in 1887, connecting Northampton through Hadley and Amherst to points east, it was threading itself into one of the most densely settled agricultural landscapes in New England.<sup>[1]</sup>

The railroad carried what the valley produced: passengers commuting between the Five College towns, freight moving between the farms of Hadley and the warehouses of Northampton, grain destined for the farmer's supply warehouse in Amherst. Three round-trip passenger trains ran daily in the 1920s, alongside numerous freight trains.<sup>[1]</sup> The line was leased to the Boston & Maine Railroad, which called it the Central Massachusetts Branch, and for a generation it was as unremarkable and essential as the roads.

Then the roads won. Passenger service ended on April 23, 1932, as the automobile made the short trips between Pioneer Valley towns faster and cheaper.<sup>[1]</sup> Freight held on longer, sustained by the paper mill in Northampton — but when the mill closed around 1979, the last reason to run trains through here disappeared.<sup>[1]</sup> The state acquired the corridor in 1980.

The trail's name came from a suggestion by the Hadley Historical Commission: Norwottuck, for the Native American people who had lived in this valley for thousands of years before European contact and long before any railroad.<sup>[3]</sup> Construction began in 1992, and on July 29, 1993, the Massachusetts Department of Environmental Management officially opened the Norwottuck Rail Trail.<sup>[2]</sup>

Today the trail is best known for its crossing of the Connecticut River — a graceful bridge with views of the floodplain meadows of Hadley, fields that have been cultivated for centuries. Beaver ponds near the eastern end attract great blue herons and wood ducks. The Amherst College Wildlife Sanctuary borders a stretch of the route. Emily Dickinson's house in Amherst is a short walk from the trail. The landscape is entirely familiar and quietly profound.

## Historical Sites

### Site 1: Norwottuck Station Site / Historic Freight Depot (Hadley)
- **Lat/Lng:** 42.3490, -72.5440
- **Description:** The Massachusetts Central Railroad Freight Depot in Hadley survives along the trail corridor — one of the few remaining physical structures from the railroad era. A historic photograph from around 1917 shows the Norwottuck Station at South Amherst, capturing the scale and ordinariness of branch-line railroad life in this valley. The depot is listed in the Wikimedia Commons image record for this trail.
- **Image available:** "Norwottuck Station, South Amherst, Mass., Boston and Maine Railroad, about 1917.jpg" (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA)
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwottuck_Rail_Trail

### Site 2: Emily Dickinson's Amherst
- **Lat/Lng:** 42.3732, -72.5199
- **Description:** The trail passes through Amherst, where Emily Dickinson spent nearly her entire life. The Emily Dickinson Museum — the house where she wrote almost 1,800 poems — is a short walk from the trail at 280 Main Street. Dickinson is known to have observed the railroad from her property; she wrote about locomotives in several poems. The Five College corridor the trail passes through remains one of the most literarily active landscapes in New England.
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emily_Dickinson_Museum

## In-Development Trails

### Mass Central Rail Trail — Belchertown Greenway Extension
- **Status:** Active development (bridge reconstruction funded 2025)
- **Description:** The Norwottuck Rail Trail is the anchor segment of the larger Mass Central Rail Trail, a 104-mile planned corridor from Northampton to Boston. The trail is 64 miles open and 92 miles protected. Directly east of the Norwottuck terminus in Belchertown, the Belchertown Greenway segment is under active development, with MassTrails awarding a 2025 grant for reconstruction of the Jabish Brook bridge. When complete, the MCRT will allow riders to travel from Northampton to Boston almost entirely on trail.
- **Link:** https://www.masscentralrailtrail.org/

### Mass Central Rail Trail — Clinton Section
- **Status:** Active construction (2024–2025 phases)
- **Description:** Further east along the MCRT corridor, the Clinton section is in active construction in 2024–2025, with trail phases being built to improve tunnel access and connect gaps in the network. The Berlin-to-Hudson corridor received a feasibility study grant in 2024.
- **Link:** https://www.masscentralrailtrail.org/
